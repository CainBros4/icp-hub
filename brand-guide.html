Guide 02 · Style

The visual identity,
in full.

Logo brief, color palette, typography, voice, and the hero story arc. Hand this to any designer, logo generator, or creative partner and they can build on-brand.

Key Lime in one sentence
"Lemonade for real estate — a warm, modern, premium brand that makes buying and selling a home feel as easy as pie, without agents, for 1% instead of 6%."
01 · Logo Brief

Clean wordmark. Stylized lime. Warm green.

A person sees this logo and within 1 second knows: modern tech brand, warm & fresh, premium, belongs next to Lemonade and Airbnb. This is the brief to hand to a designer or logo generator.

The Playbook · Follow Lemonade

Lemonade took a boring, distrusted industry and created a brand identity that felt completely fresh. Clean, modern wordmark + simple stylized fruit icon + signature color. Icon works standalone as app icon. Wordmark works standalone. Key Lime does the exact same structure — the name already does the heavy lifting; the visual just needs to match the energy.

Icon direction

A stylized lime — cross-section slice, whole lime with leaf, or abstracted citrus wedge. Minimal and geometric — not illustrative. Recognizable at 32×32px. Ownable — not clip art. Study how Lemonade stylized their lemon.

Wordmark direction

"Key Lime" in modern geometric sans-serif — Manrope, General Sans, Outfit, or Cerebri Sans. Medium to bold weight — confident, not delicate. Generous letter-spacing. No custom letterforms, no hidden symbols, no clever tricks — the name is already clever enough.

What it must NOT be

  • A house, rooftop, door, window, or any real estate iconography — Key Lime is a tech brand, not a brokerage
  • A key shape or key integrated into letterforms — too literal, too locksmith
  • A gradient on the wordmark — keep the type clean and solid
  • More than 2 colors — the green + dark text, that's it
  • Script or decorative typeface — this is a technology company
  • Anything that looks like a juice bar, smoothie shop, or restaurant — the citrus reference must feel tech-brand, not food-service

02 · Color System

Warm green. Cream base. Savings gold.

Every color was chosen to occupy space no real estate competitor currently owns — and to trigger the right psychological response in each ICP segment.

#A8C856 Primary Brand
Key Lime Green
Logo · CTAs · Brand accents
The fresh, warm, warm-yellow-green of an actual key lime. Not neon. Not sage. Not forest. Nobody in real estate owns this — an open visual lane.
#FFF8ED Primary BG
Warm Cream
Backgrounds · Pages · Content areas
Slightly warm to avoid the clinical coldness of pure white — which would make Key Lime feel like every other proptech clone. Communicates openness, transparency, the absence of hidden complexity.
#2D3436 Headings · Text
Charcoal Slate
Wordmark · Headlines · Body text · Dark bg
Warm, not pure black. Softer and more modern — reads as premium next to the cream base without the harsh contrast of true black.
#E8A317 Savings Only
Savings Gold
Dollar figures · Savings amounts ONLY
Reserved exclusively for financial reward moments. When you see gold on a Key Lime surface, you just saved money. Never decorative, never a background.
#5BB5A2 AI / Tech
Advisor Teal
AI features · Advisor UI · Technology labels
Signals intelligence — applied exclusively to AI-powered features. When you see teal, your advisor is doing the work. Differentiates the AI layer from brand trust (green).
#D98E5A Warm Accent
Warm Terracotta
Secondary accents · Seller-leaning moments
Complementary warmth — the kitchen-at-golden-hour feeling. Used sparingly for seller psychology moments and creative accents; never competes with the brand green.

Palette rules

  1. Key Lime Green is the hero. It's the first color anyone associates with the brand.
  2. Never pure white (#FFFFFF). Always warm cream. Key Lime is warm.
  3. Never pure black (#000000). Always charcoal slate. Softer, more modern.
  4. Savings Gold is reserved exclusively for dollar amounts. Never decorative.
  5. The palette should feel like walking into a sunlit modern kitchen with natural wood and green plants — warm, fresh, clean.

Colors to avoid — and why

Each one would cause a specific psychological misfire with the ICP.

Red
In financial contexts, red = losses. Owned by RE/MAX, KW — signals establishment.
Navy Blue
Old-guard banking and Zillow/Redfin. Communicates establishment, not disruption.
Purple
Signals crypto / blockchain — trust-ambiguous in a high-stakes transaction.
Spotify Green
Too neon, too techy. Key Lime's green is warmer and softer — not electric.
Gold as primary
Too Century 21 — signals old-money establishment, the opposite of disruptor positioning.
Pure White BG
Clinical, indistinguishable from every Zillow/Redfin/Opendoor clone. Warm cream is the differentiator.

04 · Archetype Stack

Outlaw. Magician. Everyperson.

Three archetypes running simultaneously — the combination is the signature. Any one alone would fail. Seller-side adds Caregiver, Sovereign, Mature Outlaw, Concrete Magician — those live in the Messaging guide.

⚔️
Primary · Positioning

The Outlaw

Disruption · Revelation
"The rules are broken. Here's what's actually possible."
Disrupts what isn't working. Core fear: being powerless, being exploited by the system. Avoid the shadow — cynicism, anger for its own sake.
✨
Secondary · Product

The Magician

Transformation · Demonstration
"What seemed impossible is now effortless."
Makes dreams reality. Core fear: shallow trickery. Avoid the shadow — "too good to be true" distrust. Peer brands: Apple, Stripe, Airbnb.
🤝
Tertiary · Tone

The Everyperson

Belonging · Accessibility
"Normal people are already doing this. You belong."
Belonging without exclusion. Core fear: being out of their depth, joining a cult of disruptors. Avoid blandness.

The combination

Outlaw positioning (the system is broken) delivered with Magician composure (and here's the tool that makes it irrelevant), in an Everyperson tone (and anyone can use it). Operational shorthand: "Confident inevitability."

05 · Voice · Alex

Your smartest friend who happens to know real estate.

Key Lime's voice is Alex — a 35-year-old Tampa resident who has done multiple transactions, works in tech or finance, got tired of watching money leave at closings, and is now the knowledgeable friend explaining things clearly and pulling no punches.

Alex shows the math instead of asking for trust. Confident without arrogance. Warm without being saccharine. Treats you like a capable adult.
Alex DOES say
  • "Here's what that actually costs you."
  • "Let me show you the math."
  • "Yes — fully licensed under Florida law."
  • "Your advisor countered at $437K. Here's why."
  • "Keep the difference."
Alex NEVER says
  • "It's complicated."
  • "Trust the process."
  • "Our team of experts…"
  • "Easy," "Discount," "Cheap," "FSBO"

06 · Brand Story Arc

The customer is Luke. Key Lime is Yoda.

Every homepage, ad, and email should map to this arc. The customer makes the bold choice — Key Lime equips them to win. The villain is the broken commission system, never agents as individuals.

Act 1 · The World As It Is

The commission system was built against you

"Every year, millions of Americans hand $20,000–$50,000 to real estate agents at closing. Most never knew they were paying the buyer's agent's fee too. The system was built by agents, for agents — and the rules haven't changed in decades."
The "before state." Name the injustice clearly so the ICP feels seen. 49% didn't know they paid the buyer's agent. 67% believe agents prioritize commissions over outcomes. Surface these facts without being preachy — let the numbers do the work.
Act 2 · The Call to Change

The crack in the system — and the third option

"The NAR settlement cracked it open. AI made it possible. Now you don't have to choose between a 5.5% agent and a risky FSBO. There's a third option."
Introduce Key Lime as the answer the disruption created. The Magician archetype dominates — show the data, show the math, prove the platform works. This is where the savings calculator lives. Address the top objection: "This is too new for the biggest purchase of my life." Counter with licensed credibility + AI track record + human escalation path.
Act 3 · The New World

You closed. You kept what you earned.

"Buyers and sellers using Key Lime keep their equity. The advisor negotiates with precision. A local chaperone shows the home. You close at 1%."
The transformation state. Testimonials, real savings numbers, named case studies. Specificity is critical: "$18,200 saved by Sarah K., Westchase" converts. "Save thousands" does not. The chaperone model appears here as proof that human connection was never sacrificed — only the unnecessary middleman was removed.
The Call to Action

The customer becomes the hero — right now

The story doesn't end with inspiration. It ends with a specific, low-commitment action that delivers immediate value. The savings calculator is the primary entry point — it produces a personalized number that makes the value proposition undeniable before any signup.
