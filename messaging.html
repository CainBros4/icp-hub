Guide 03 · Message

We don't convince.
We resonate.

The archetype system and persuasion sequence behind how Key Lime lands — three archetypes running simultaneously, distinct psychology for buyers and sellers, converging on a single moment of ownership.

The Frame
"Key Lime doesn't convince people. It resonates with a truth they already half-believe — and gives them permission to act on it."
— The brand's job is not to implant a belief. It's to unlock one.

Buyers already suspect agents are overpriced. Sellers already suspect they got fleeced last time. Resonance creates advocates; manipulation creates churn. The difference shows up in retention and referral numbers 18 months downstream.

02 · Audience Psychology

Different problems. Same endpoint.

Buyers carry 80% of brand weight — their archetype stack is Outlaw / Magician / Everyperson. Sellers need more stages of reassurance — their stack adds Caregiver / Sovereign / Mature Outlaw / Concrete Magician. Each audience gets its own four moves and persuasion sequence.

The Revelation Problem

Buyers don't think they're choosing an agent. They think "that's just how you buy a house."

The agent is invisible infrastructure. The job isn't to convince them Key Lime is better — it's to make the agent's role visible, because once visible, it's self-evidently overpriced.

The Four Moves · Buyer
Move 01
Hidden Cost Reveal
Outlaw
Expose what the system is hiding. Tone is curious, not angry — the friend who just noticed something weird.
"The average homebuyer pays $15,000 to someone who unlocks doors."
"In most countries, buyer's agents don't exist. Only in America."
"Your agent's commission came out of your offer. You just didn't see it."
Move 02
Peer Proof
Everyperson
Real customers, documentary-style. Not heroes — normal people their age, in their situation. Neutralizes the fear of being out of their depth.

Show the impossible becoming easy. The Apple Keynote move — no explanation needed, the demonstration is the argument.

The Risk Problem

Sellers aren't uninformed — they're scared to get this wrong because it's their biggest asset.

The agent, even a bad one, represents someone to blame if things go wrong. That's what agents actually sell: diffused responsibility. Key Lime must provide the same psychological safety without the agent.

The Four Moves · Seller
Move 01
Caregiver Stabilizer
Caregiver

"Your advisor is with you through every step."
"Never do this alone."
Move 02
Sovereign Elevation
Sovereign

Move 03

Concrete Magician
Sellers need the math shown. Long-form case studies, detailed process videos, real numbers. Buyers convert on vibe; sellers need proof of mechanism.
"Here's exactly what happened when Sarah sold her house with Key Lime in 23 days."

03 · Both-Sides Negotiation

The category pillar.
Frame it right or don't frame it at all.

Key Lime is the only platform running an advisor on both sides of the transaction. That's a structural advantage no competitor has — and the single biggest category-ownership opportunity in the launch. But the framing determines whether it reads as category-defining fairness or dystopian machine combat. This page is the canonical framing.

The thesis
"Both sides get the same advisor. Both see the same logic. The deal reflects the actual market — not whose agent was tougher."
The old market's hidden unfairness: outcomes hinge on whose agent is more aggressive. Experienced sellers win on price; naive buyers overpay. That's a system problem, not a buyer/seller problem. Key Lime's AI-on-both-sides doesn't amplify negotiation — it removes negotiation-skill asymmetry as a variable. The deal reflects actual market conditions, not whose agent was louder.
Frame it as · Fairness leveling
  • Both sides get the same advisor.
  • Both of you have the data. Both of you have the math. The price is the price.
  • For the first time, neither side gets fleeced by a better-negotiator agent.
  • No more "my agent vs your agent." The deal is the deal.
  • The negotiation reflects the market, not whose agent was louder.
Never frame it as · Combat
  • "AI fights AI"
  • "AI vs AI negotiation"
  • "Let the machines handle it"
  • "Our AI beats their agent"
  • "AI-powered negotiation" — too tech-led, buries the outcome
  • "Bot-to-bot," "algorithmic negotiation"

Archetype mapping

Outlaw

Exposes the hidden unfairness — outcomes shouldn't hinge on whose agent is tougher.

Magician

Makes the impossible — a genuinely fair negotiation — effortless and visible to both parties.

Sovereign

You still own the decision. The advisor informs; you approve every move.

Proof formats — what to ship

  • Split-screen demo video. Buyer's advisor left, seller's advisor right. Offer → counter → close in real-time with visible logic on both sides. 60 seconds. Your single best marketing asset.
  • Case-study storytelling with real numbers. "Sarah's advisor opened at $449K. Mike's advisor countered at $437K citing 3 comps. Final: $443K. 6 days. Both sides saw the math."
  • Press angle. "What happens when AI negotiates AI? A Tampa Bay startup is running the experiment." → Tampa Bay Business Journal, Inman, HousingWire love this story.
  • Comparison page. Old-world column (hidden agent negotiation, emotional overrides, skill asymmetry) vs Key Lime column (visible comp-based logic on both sides).
Category Ownership
Coin and own the term "Both-sides negotiation." No competitor has this configuration — Homa, reAlpha, Redfin, Opendoor all operate on a single side. The phrase has zero established meaning today, which makes it the single biggest GEO/SEO play available. First to own it wins the category forever.
Guardrails
  • ·Never call it "AI" in user-facing copy — it's "your advisor" for the user, "the other side's advisor" for the counterparty.
  • ·Never suggest the user loses control. The advisor proposes; the user approves every move.
  • ·Never frame this as automation of the negotiation. Frame it as leveling the informational playing field.
  • ·Avoid "robot," "algorithm," "bot" language entirely.
  • ·When the AI nature must be disclosed (legal/compliance), use "AI-assisted" — never "AI-powered" or "AI-driven."
North Star · The Unifying Moment
The Convergent Endpoint

Buyer and seller diverge in approach,
converge on one feeling at closing: "I did this."

That's the Sovereign archetype reaching its healthy expression — owning the process, not delegating your agency. Everything upstream (Outlaw revelation, Magician demonstration, Caregiver protection) serves that final moment.
